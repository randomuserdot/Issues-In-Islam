## PART TWO: COMMON ARGUMENTS/MISCONCEPTIONS



Now, you may have seen the istiqlāli argument, saying: “If you say that duʿāʾ is apart of worship, and thus you say that duʿāʾ to the dead in matters that is obviously šhirk, why wouldn't you call the duʿāʾ of Aḥmad to the angels to be polytheism?"

We say:
This is a misunderstanding of what duʿāʾ is, aswell as what necessitates šhirk. This is like seeking a request from the Jinn, they live in a reality which is unknown to us, thus we cannot see them, but they can hear us and interact with our world. 

The dead are categorically incapable of offering such assistance in the worldly realm by their own capacity. Those who invoke the dead for help explicitly attribute to them powers such as hearing supplications from afar, responding across distances, granting children, curing illnesses in supernatural ways, providing sustenance, removing harm, and intervening in affairs that belong exclusively to Allāh.


Calling upon the dead does not simply involve seeking help from an absent or hidden being but entails affirming for them divine or quasi-divine attributes. It is precisely this attribution of powers that only Allāh possesses, which renders such acts major šhirk, regardless of whether the practitioner pays lip service to tawḥīd or claims a mediating intent.

In contrast, if the jinns are living and are morally responsible beings who act, move and hear, this applies to the angels with no contradiction. Angels are already morally grounded and are the literal servants of our Lord, thus asking a request from them, just like Aḥmad did, is not polytheism at all. Rather, it is one of the permissible forms and we see no harm in it, as the literal request of Aḥmad was for direction to a physical location as he got lost.

This is not a mundane task for the angels, therefore this all stems from your misunderstanding of what a "mundane task" is and how we differentiate between the dead and the living.

“The Qurayšh used to circumambulate the House while they were naked, whistling and clapping.”

So making these actions, which contradict humility and submission, into worship indicates that it is not correct to interpret worship merely by its linguistic meaning. Rather, it is necessary to add the condition of belief in lordship for an act to be considered worship.

However, using this proof to claim that the concept of worship and šhirk is restricted to belief in lordship alone is incorrect. Even if we concede that this verse indicates that the polytheists used to worship Allāh through whistling and clapping, the verse does not describe for us the specific manner of that act.

It is known that not every instance of whistling and clapping contradicts humility and submission some forms and conditions of it may involve reverence and humility.


There are groups among the polytheists who worship through dancing, beating drums, and music, and they perform these in an organised manner without noise or disturbance. Rather, some of its melodies may lead to humility and even weeping.


Thus, it is possible that the clapping and whistling of the disbelievers of the Qurayšh was of this type especially since they used to do it during ṭawāf, as Ibn ʿAbbās mentioned.


Even if it is said that they performed these actions in a way that contradicts humility and submission, this would not be surprising from them. For if they deviated in the aṣl of worship by directing it to other than Allāh, then it is natural that they would deviate in the particulars of worship, establishing matters that contradict reason and revelation.

And Allāh’s describing their action as “prayer” was not in approval of them or in affirming that name for it; rather, it was in the context of condemnation and rebuke. Thus, it is not correct to say that the Qurʾān affirmed the disbelievers in making whistling and clapping into worship, or that their action is truly considered prayer, rather, this naming was according to what they themselves claimed.

Therefore, the Qurʾān is condemning the disbelievers of Qurayšh for inventing types of worship for which Allāh sent down no authority.

Even if it is said that the Arab polytheists only considered whistling and clapping as worship due to their intention and purpose, this still does not prove restricting the concept of worship in its entirety to that.

Therefore, the istiqlālis cannot rebuild their argument in the issue of defining worship to be only "obedience" and its branches as some have claimed. 


They, the istiqlālis, also argue that the polytheists did šhirk in ar-rubūbiyyah, thus our arguments are very irrelevant. However, this is a flaw and categorical error on their part.

I will be quoting from a brother who made a in-depth analysis on this similar issue in response to a misconception.

Br. Zuhayr said:

“Imām al-Ṭabarī, said in his commentary on “He who made the earth a resting place for you and the sky a canopy, and sent down from the sky water by which He brought forth for you fruits as provision so do not set up rivals to Allah while you know [better].” After presenting the various interpretations of this verse, he noted that Mujāhid understood the intended recipients of the verse to be the People of the Book:



“And I consider that the one who called for this interpretation, Mujāhid, and added to that the fact that it is an address to the People of the Torah and the Gospel [i.e., Jews and Christians] and not to others [he assumed] that the Arabs did not know that Allah is their Creator and Provider, and by their denial they reject the tawḥīd [oneness] of their Lord and associate with Him in worship others besides Him. And indeed, that is a statement [it is shocking]! But Allah, exalted be His praise, has informed in His Book about them [the Arabs] that they acknowledged His tawḥīd, yet they associated in His worship what they associated [with Him]. He said, ‘And if you ask them who created them, they will say, Allah’ [Quran 29:61], and He said, ‘Say, “Who provides for you from the heavens and the earth? Or who controls hearing and sight? Or who brings forth the living from the dead and the dead from the living? Or who governs the affair?” They will say, “Allah.” Say, “Then will you not fear [Him]?”

So the one that is more appropriate for the interpretation of His saying: ‘And you know’ because what existed among the Arabs regarding knowledge of Allah’s tawḥīd, and that He is the Originator of creation, their Creator, and Provider, was similar to what existed among the People of the Scriptures. And there is no indication in the verse that Allah, exalted be His praise, intended by ‘And you know’ [to refer to] one of the two parties [i.e., either the Arabs or the People of the Book]; rather, the address by this means is general to all people, because He challenged all people with His words: ‘O people, worship your Lord.’

Therefore, its interpretation is not restricted to what Ibn ʿAbbās and Qatāda said [that it refers to every morally responsible person who knows Allah’s tawḥīd and that He has no partner in His creation, yet associates others with Him in worship], but rather [it applies] to all people, whoever they may be.”



Tafsīr al-Ṭabarī, vol 1, p. 394, ed. al-Turki, 2001, Dar Hajar, Cairo, Egypt.



I comment: What Imām al-Ṭabarī is stating here is very clear. He establishes tawḥīd al-rubūbiyyah and tawḥīd al-ulūhiyyah as having distinct entailments and rulings. The polytheists’ affirmation of tawḥīd al-rubūbiyyah in its basic form did not benefit them, because they denied the full reality and necessary implications of tawḥīd, such as directing worship exclusively to Allah.



He explicitly attributes to the polytheists of Quraysh an acknowledgment of oneness (waḥdāniyyah). He states unequivocally: “But Allah, exalted be His praise, has informed in His Book about them [the Arabs] that they acknowledged His tawḥīd, yet they associated in His worship what they associated [with Him].” (ولكنّ الله جلّ ثناؤه قد أخبرَ في كتابه عنها أنها كانت تُقر بوحدانيته، غير أنها كانت تشرك في عبادته ما كانت تشرك فيها).



This demonstrates al-Ṭabarī’s explicit characterization of tawḥīd as having two distinct meanings and implications. Even though he does not formally label [nor try to establish a taxonomy] these as separate categories, his treatment of rubūbiyyah as distinct from ulūhiyyah in terms of rulings is sufficient to establish a real distinction. Here, the doctrinal entailments of the divisions of tawḥīd are plainly evident.



Consider also how he adduces other verses to establish that the polytheists of Quraysh committed explicit shirk in ulūhiyyah through their acts themselves. He does not consider this as denial of creative lordship.

These doctrinal entailments make it clear that al-Ṭabarī does not regard belief in creatorship and provision (al-khāliqiyyah) as a prerequisite for classifying an act directed toward an object as worship (ʿibādah). Rather, a polytheist may combine both positions: affirming oneness in creatorship while simultaneously committing polytheism in worship (al-ʿibādah).



Thus, regardless of how tawḥīd may later be formally categorized, al-Ṭabarī had no difficulty affirming that the polytheists of Quraysh possessed tawḥīd in one respect and shirk in another. He did not attempt to systematize their beliefs into rigid categories; instead, he operated on the basis of doctrinal essence and its entailments rather than formal classification.



These were polytheists (mushrikūn) whose belief did not benefit them, nor did it prevent revelation from describing them as “worshippers of other than Allah.” It therefore becomes clear that Imām al-Ṭabarī employed a criterion other than the notion that tawḥīd is a one-dimensional doctrinal ruling.

Imām al-Ṭabarī further says in another place:



“He [i.e., Allah] — exalted is His mention — says: Most of those whom the Exalted described by His statement, “How many a sign there is in the heavens and the earth which they pass by while turning away from it” do not affirm belief in Allah as the One who created them, provides for them, and is the Creator of all things, except while they are associating partners [with Him] (al-shirk). This [association] occurs through their worship of idols and images, their taking lords besides Him (arbāb [objects of devotion and authority]), and their claim that He has a child. Exalted is Allah above what they assert.”



Tafsīr al-Ṭabarī, vol 13, p. 372, ed. al-Turki, 2001, Dar Hajar, Cairo, Egypt.



I comment: Once again, al-Ṭabarī distinguishes between affirming Allah’s oneness in creation and provision (tawḥīd al-khalq wa ar-rizq) and the act of worshiping other than Allah (ʿibādah li-ghayr Allāh). He does not make the former a condition for the realization of the latter. Rather, a polytheist may combine both: affirming Allah’s exclusive creatorship while associating others with Him in worship. The first belief does not negate the validity of describing the second act as ʿibādah. Thus, a person may be a muwaḥḥid with respect to rubūbiyyah and, at the same time, a mushrik in worship (ʿibādah).



We also observe that al-Ṭabarī did not distinguish, in usage, between the terms ilāh (god) and rabb (lord). He frequently used rabb to mean ilāh and vice versa. This is because the two are inseparable: when distinguished, they imply one another, and when combined, each retains a distinct meaning, similar to the relationship between Īmān (faith) and Islām (submission). Therefore, what matters in the division into lordship and divinity is the underlying meaning, not the mere labeling of one as “lordship” and the other as “divinity.” One should not object to this classification simply because the word “lord” or “lords” appears in revelation or in scholars’ statements to signify “god.” Each term may be used to refer to the other without any problem for the soundness of the classification, whose meaning is firmly established.



I believe these two quotes are sufficient to establish the distinction between rubūbiyyah and ulūhiyyah within the corpus of Imam al-Ṭabarī. However, in the book version, I will provide additional quotations from Imam al-Ṭabarī on this issue.



Imām al-Ṭabarī On Polytheistic Intercession:


Imām al-Ṭabarī says:

He exalted be His mention says: “And those who have taken [as] protectors (awliyāʾ) other than Allah, they follow them [and] worship them besides Allah, saying to them: ‘We do not worship you, O gods, except that you may bring us closer to Allah [in nearness] (zulfā), in rank and station, and intercede for us with Him in our needs…’”

Regarding those who said this, it is narrated: Muhammad ibn ʿAmr narrated to us… on the authority of Mujāhid regarding His statement: “We do not worship them except that they may bring us closer to Allah [in nearness] (zulfā),” he said: “The Quraysh say this to the idols, and those before them said it to the angels, to ʿĪsā son of Maryam, and to ʿAzīr [allegedly a prophet].”



Tafsīr al-Ṭabarī, vol 20, p. 156, ed. al-Turki, 2001, Dar Hajar, Cairo, Egypt.



I comment: This establishes that the polytheists directed acts of worship toward their deities not with the aim of managing them, deriving benefit from them, or causing harm in their affairs independently (istiqlāl) or through creation and creative power (ibdāʿ). Rather, they sought to attain nearness to Allah and to seek intercession (shafā‘ah) through these deities for their needs.

Al-Ṭabarī here demonstrates once again that merely labeling them as “worshippers of other than Allah” (ʿābidīn li-ghayr Allah) and their deities as “gods” (ālīhah) suffices for directing certain acts toward them, even if the polytheists believe only in their intercessory role (shafā‘ah al-shirkī) and not in their independent capacity to create, provide sustenance, manage affairs, or act autonomously. It is sufficient that certain acts are directed toward them so that they may intercede with Allah in fulfilling needs, thereby rendering them “worshipped” (ʿābidah) and “gods” (ālīhah).

In Mujāhid’s tafsīr, polytheistic intercession (shafā‘ah al-shirkī) is extended to include the intercession of Jews and Christians through their prophets and angels, despite the fact that neither Jews nor Christians claim that such intercession occurs independently of Allah’s permission, nor that it compels Him to act on behalf of those lacking sovereignty or authority.” End of his words.
